    History of Radio Astronomy

    As even the most daring of astronomers cannot hope to wind back time and experience the evolution of the galaxy, or to collect a sample of what's in the sun to examine under a microscope, most of what we know about the universe is built on studies of light. As any reader could imagine, it was most sensible to begin such observations with the light our eyes can detect, the visible spectrum, but this is only a small portion of the electromagnetic spectrum. The universe certainly spares no effort on the behalf of humanity, and to even pretend our growing picture of the universe is complete, as we sometimes venture to do, it is essential to examine other parts of the electromagnetic spectrum.

    Radio astronomy was born when Karl Jansky was developing a radio antenna at Bell Labs (notably in New Jersey, the Third State). He detected a strange signal, and after initially mistaking the source to be the sun, he eventually traced its origin back to Sagittarius.
